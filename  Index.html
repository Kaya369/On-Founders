An Essay · 2026

On Founders

A working theory of what a great founder is, built from four years of reading two hundred and fifty biographies. The sixteen psychological traits that the people who reshape the world share, why they have them, and how to spot them early.

Kaya

The thesis of this paper is simple. The people who reshape the world share a specific set of psychological traits, and most of those traits are observable long before the company exists. If you can identify them early, you can find the next great founders before the market does. If you cannot, you will spend your career investing in people the market has already priced.

I have spent the last four years reading two hundred and fifty biographies of founders trying to extract the pattern. Edison, Rockefeller, Ford, Carnegie, Jobs, Knight, Walton, Lauder, Bugatti, Mateschitz, Buffett, Munger, Simons, Singleton, Ferrari, Ellison, Gates, Dell, Chung Ju-yung, Hetty Green, Christian Dior, Kanye West, Conor McGregor, Caesar, Genghis Khan, Churchill, the Nobel brothers, and many others. I read them in airports, in cafes, in the back of taxis, in the corner of the gym before training. I read the same lives two and three times because I was looking for something I could not yet name.

I can name it now. There are about sixteen traits that show up consistently across these lives. They appear in different combinations, in different intensities, and in different mediums. Some of these people built companies. Some built empires. Some built brands. Some built ideas. The underlying psychology is the same.

A note on tone before we start. This is not a celebration. Most of these people were difficult, many were unpleasant, several would not have been good company. The point is not that we should admire them. The point is that they share a specific psychological profile, and if you understand that profile, you can do two things: find them earlier than other investors, and recognise the shape if it ever shows up in yourself.

Each section that follows is structured the same way. A definition of the trait. A few short proofs from the biographies, with the founders' own words. A psychological hypothesis for why they have it. And a diagnostic question, which is how I now use the trait to look for the people who might one day be in a book like the ones I have been reading.

The conclusion is a single page. A working checklist of all sixteen, with the diagnostic for each.

The traits are the evidence. The biographies are the proof.
Trait One

Obsession

The operating state of every great founder. It is not interest. It is not even passion. It is the inability to think about anything else.

Conor McGregor is the clearest articulation. He said it on camera, and he keeps saying it.

There is no talent here. This is hard work. This is an obsession. I am not talented. I am obsessed.Conor McGregor

Thomas Edison slept on the lab floor for weeks at a time. Warren Buffett, at ninety-five, still reads five hundred pages a day. Charlie Munger died at ninety-nine with a stack of biographies on the bedside table. Enzo Ferrari worked eleven hours a day, seven days a week, into his eighties. Ettore Bugatti designed his own egg-cooker because the one he had bought was not good enough.

Where it comes from

Obsession is rarely a choice. It is a wiring. The founders who have it almost always describe being unable to think about anything else, often from a young age. Edison loved chemistry before he was eight. Michael Dell took apart his first Apple at fifteen. The pattern is closer to a fixation than a decision. The work picks them as much as they pick the work.

How to spot it

They cannot stop talking about their domain. They have done thousands of hours of voluntary work in it by the time you meet them. They will keep working when there is no audience, no money, and no encouragement. Interest stops when the friction starts. Obsession does not notice the friction.

Trait Two

The Wound

A formative pain, usually inflicted early, that the founder converts into fuel rather than excuse.

Steve Jobs was given up for adoption by biological parents who could have kept him. Phil Knight's father called him a disgrace for selling shoes after Stanford. Les Schwab's father drank himself to death in a ditch when Schwab was twelve. Andre Agassi's father strapped him to a ball machine at age three and made him hit two thousand five hundred balls a day. Francis Coppola's father told him there could only be one genius in the family. Coppola later won three Oscars and hired his father to score The Godfather Part II.

Charlie Munger lost his nine-year-old son to leukaemia. He read Epictetus the year after. He concluded:

Self-pity has no utility.Charlie Munger
Where it comes from

The wound usually happens before the founder has the cognitive tools to process it. They then spend the rest of their life routing around it. The company, the empire, the brand: these are the architectures they build to deal with what they could not process directly. Munger called it routing through. David Senra calls it the fuel that does not run out. Freud would have called it sublimation. They are describing the same thing.

How to spot it

Founders rarely volunteer the wound, but they cannot help referencing it. Listen for the parent mentioned with too much detachment. The childhood story described as ordinary but containing something extraordinary. The decision early in life that looks irrational from the outside. The chip on the shoulder, even when the founder is otherwise polished.

I have come to think of this as the most important trait in the stack. It is the one that determines whether the engine will still be running in twenty years. Talent runs out. Money runs out. The wound does not.

Trait Three

Agency

The refusal to behave as if the world is fixed. Marc Andreessen calls it understanding that the world is more malleable than people think.

Most people grow up assuming the world is the way it is. The great founders refuse this assumption. They behave as if the world is wet clay, and they are the ones with their hands on it.

Chung Ju-yung wanted to build a shipyard for South Korea. The country had no shipbuilding industry. He went to Barclays in London with no factory, no contracts, and no track record. He pulled a five-hundred-won note out of his pocket showing a sixteenth-century Korean warship and told the bankers that Korea had built ships before England did. He got the loan. He built the largest shipyard in the world. When his team later told him a tidal current was too strong to block during a land reclamation project, he sank an old oil tanker in the gap. It worked. His standing question to anyone who told him a thing was impossible:

Have you ever tried?Chung Ju-yung

Dietrich Mateschitz did not enter the energy drink market because there was no energy drink market. He invented the category. Julius Caesar narrated his own conquests in the third person, in the present tense, as he was making them. The dispatches arrived in Rome at the same time as the victories.

Where it comes from

Agency is part innate, part learned. The founders who have it usually had an early experience of changing the world around them. They moved a thing, asked for a thing, broke a thing, won a thing, and the world rearranged itself. They never forgot the lesson. Andreessen has said that one of the things he looks for in a founder is whether they have already done this in small ways. The micro-agency in adolescence becomes the macro-agency in business.

How to spot it

They describe the world as fixable. They have agency over their own life. They have already moved cities, started businesses, learned skills nobody asked them to learn. They do not wait for permission. They never have.

Trait Four

First-Principles Thinking

The ability to reason from fundamental truths rather than from analogy or convention. To break a problem down to its irreducible parts and rebuild the answer from base reality.

Most people reason by analogy. X is like Y, so we should do what Y did. This is fast, and at the frontier it is almost always wrong. The great founders reason by decomposition. They take a problem, strip it down to physics, economics, or human nature, and reconstruct the answer from the ground up. The result usually looks insane to people who reason by analogy, which is almost everyone.

The cleanest example is Elon Musk's approach to the cost of a rocket. The space industry consensus in 2002 was that rockets were expensive because rockets were expensive. Musk asked a different question. What are the raw materials in a rocket, and what do they cost on the commodities market? The answer was about two percent of the price NASA was paying. Everything else was markup. He concluded a rocket could be built fifty times cheaper than it was being built, and he set out to prove it. SpaceX is the proof.

Reason from first principles rather than by analogy.Elon Musk

Henry Ford asked the same kind of question in 1908. Every other manufacturer was building luxury cars for the wealthy because that is what cars were. Ford asked why. The answer was assembly: cars were expensive because they were built by hand. He built the first moving assembly line, sold the Model T for half the price, and made himself the richest man in America. Rockefeller asked why oil was chaotic and fragmented. The answer was that no one had integrated the supply chain. He integrated it. Edwin Land asked why a photograph required a darkroom. He removed the darkroom. Jim Simons asked why a hedge fund needed traders from Wall Street.

The only way to get ahead is to find errors in conventional wisdom.Larry Ellison
Where it comes from

First-principles thinking is built, not given. Most people are trained from childhood to defer to consensus, because consensus is the default of any group. The founders who develop the trait usually do so through some kind of intellectual training that punishes bad reasoning quickly: mathematics, physics, engineering, philosophy. Bezos was an electrical engineer. Musk has a physics degree. Simons was a topologist. Munger trained in law and read physics for pleasure. The disciplines that produce first-principles thinkers are the ones that will not let you cheat.

How to spot it

Ask them why something in their industry is the way it is. The convention-thinkers will give you a list of reasons that boil down to "that is how it is done." The first-principles thinkers will start somewhere unexpected, usually with a physical, economic, or psychological fundamental, and build forward. The diagnostic is the structure of the answer, not the content. First-principles reasoning has a shape. It moves from the bottom up, not from sideways across to a similar example.

Trait Five

Disagreeableness

The willingness to say what others will not, knowing they will not. Comfort with the social cost of being right when the room is wrong.

Most people, even smart people, will not disagree out loud with the group they are in. The cost is real. Conformity is rewarded. Disagreement is punished. The founders who change the world are people who have decided the cost is worth paying, and who have usually paid it many times before they ever start the company.

Steve Jobs would tell people, to their faces, that their work was terrible. He did this in front of other people. He did this in meetings. He did this in public. He was correct most of the time, and the products of Apple between 1997 and 2011 are the proof. He understood that the alternative, letting bad work pass, was worse than the social cost of saying so.

Henry Singleton built Teledyne by ignoring Wall Street's growth-at-all-costs orthodoxy. When everyone else in the conglomerate era was issuing more stock, Singleton bought back ninety percent of his own outstanding shares because the price was low and the business was good. Wall Street called him irrational. Charlie Munger called him the most rational person he had ever met. Munger's own line is the trait expressed at its deepest level.

Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome.Charlie Munger

He said it in rooms where everyone wanted to talk about strategy or vision or culture. He kept saying it because everyone in those rooms had financial incentives they were ignoring. He was disagreeable in the most useful possible way. He kept naming the thing nobody wanted named.

Where it comes from

Disagreeableness is usually paid for in childhood. The founders who have it almost always have a story of being punished, socially, for telling the truth too early. A teacher who wanted compliance. A peer group that wanted conformity. A parent who could not handle being corrected. They learned, before they had the cognitive tools to rationalise it, that other people did not actually want the truth. They kept telling it anyway. By the time they are running a company, they have already been doing this for twenty years.

How to spot it

Push back on something they have said. The disagreeable ones will defend the position. The agreeable ones will adjust. Watch for the founder who tells you, in a first meeting, that you are wrong about something. That is the trait. The opposite, the founder who tells you everything you say is a great idea, is the yellow flag. The world has plenty of yes-people. It does not need more from the founder seat.

Trait Six

The Refusal to Quit

The capacity to continue past the moment quitting becomes the rational move.

Edison tested ten thousand filaments before he found the one that would burn long enough to be a light bulb.

I have not failed. I've just found ten thousand ways that won't work.Thomas Edison

Phil Knight ran Nike for six years without a single full-time employee, working as a CPA during the day to keep the shoe company alive at night. Sam Walton lost his lease after five years of building a single five-and-dime, moved his family to Bentonville, and started over. Walmart did not begin until he was forty-four. Alfred Nobel kept manufacturing nitroglycerin after his younger brother Emil was killed in a factory accident, and rebuilt the operation on a barge on Lake Mälaren when the city of Stockholm banned him. Churchill led a country that had given up. At Harrow in 1941:

Never give in. Never give in. Never, never, never.Winston Churchill
Where it comes from

The refusal to quit almost always traces back to something psychologically prior. The founders who have it had to refuse to quit as children. A parent who left. A school that did not want them. A country they had to escape. Chung Ju-yung tried to escape the farm five times before he made it stick. Simons lost two sons in separate accidents and kept working. They had already practised the refusal in conditions much harder than the company would ever throw at them.

How to spot it

Look at their history. Have they ever stopped? Have they finished anything that took more than three years? Have they endured something they could have walked away from but chose not to? Founders who have quit other things tend to quit companies. Founders who have never quit tend to stay until the thing is done.

Trait Seven

Clock Speed and Pattern Recognition

The combination of high processing speed and a deep, well-organised library of analogies drawn from history, science, biographies, and art.

Charlie Munger built his entire investment philosophy by treating his mind as a collection of mental models drawn from physics, biology, psychology, mathematics, and history. He called it a latticework. He ran every decision through the latticework before he made it.

I have known no wise people who didn't read all the time. None. Zero.Charlie Munger

Buffett reads five hundred pages a day and has for sixty years. Jim Simons cracked Soviet codes during the Cold War before he applied the same pattern recognition to financial markets. Bezos has said he reads constantly because the most important thing in business is not knowing the answer but knowing the right question.

Where it comes from

High clock speed is largely innate. The pattern library is built. Great founders almost always have a large, well-organised store of analogies. When a new situation appears, they do not reason from scratch. They reach into the library, find the closest pattern, and act. This is why so many of them read so heavily. The reading is not for pleasure. It is for raw material.

How to spot it

Ask them about something outside their domain. The great ones will have a structured opinion within ninety seconds, supported by examples drawn from at least two unrelated fields. The slow ones will reason from first principles in real time, which is impressive but tells you the library is small.

The diagnostic question: how many books have you read this year?

Trait Eight

Self-Belief Before Evidence

The willingness to claim, out loud, an outcome the founder has not yet earned, and then to organise their life around making the claim true.

McGregor was fighting in Dublin regional shows for two-hundred-euro purses when he started telling the world he would be a two-weight UFC champion. He had not won anything yet. He talked his way to the UFC. He predicted the round he would knock out José Aldo. Aldo had been undefeated in MMA for ten years. McGregor knocked him out in thirteen seconds.

Michael Dell told his parents at fourteen that he was going to start a computer company. They sent him to the University of Texas to become a doctor. He hid his stock of computer parts under his dorm bed when they visited. He was nineteen when he started selling out of his dorm room. He was twenty-seven when he became the youngest CEO ever of a Fortune 500 company. He had been narrating the same story since he was a teenager.

Kanye West was told no by every major label he approached as a rapper. He kept claiming, in interviews and on records, that he was the greatest of all time. The work eventually caught up to the claim.

I am the greatest artist of all time.Kanye West

The Andreessen framing is sharp. Most people wait to be picked. Founders pick themselves.

Where it comes from

Self-belief before evidence is sometimes a wound (people did not believe in me so I had to) and sometimes a wiring (I have always assumed I was right). Often both. What unites the founders is that they decided, early, they did not need external validation to start. They started anyway. The validation came later, or did not, and it did not matter.

How to spot it

Have they made claims about themselves that have not yet been justified? Are they comfortable saying out loud what they intend to do, knowing they have not earned the right yet? The diagnostic is not whether they sound delusional. It is whether the delusion is precise. Sloppy self-belief sounds like noise. Founder self-belief sounds like a forecast.

Trait Nine

The Long Time Horizon

The capacity to play games whose payoffs are decades out, and to remain calm during the years between the work and the result.

Bezos famously focuses on what does not change. People will always want cheaper prices, faster delivery, and wider selection. He built Amazon around bets that take fifteen years to mature. Buffett has compounded capital for seventy-five years. His preferred holding period is forever.

The big money is not in the buying or selling. It is in the waiting.Charlie Munger

Ferrari worked into his nineties because nothing he wanted to build could be done in less than a lifetime. Walton spent five years studying retail before he made his first major move. He was not in a hurry because he was playing a game that ran for forty years.

Where it comes from

Long time horizons are partly temperamental and partly absorbed. The founders who play long games usually study people who played long games. Munger said you can read your way into the long view. Biographies act as a vaccine against short-term thinking. You see, again and again, that the great fortunes were built over decades, not quarters.

How to spot it

Ask them what they will be doing in ten years. The short-horizon people will give you a list of optimisations. The long-horizon people will describe a single project with patience baked in. Ask them about a recent decision they delayed. The short-horizon people will be embarrassed. The long-horizon people will tell you they waited deliberately.

The Andreessen frame: the difference between a good founder and a great one is often just the time horizon. The great ones are playing in slow motion while the world plays in real time.

Trait Ten

Service as the Source of Money

The understanding that money is the receipt for service rendered to the world, not the goal of the work.

Henry Ford, in 1919, owned a hundred percent of the Ford Motor Company. He had bought out every investor. He was the richest man in the country. He kept building cars for thirty more years. When asked how he had built the largest company in America, he gave the same answer he had given for forty years.

Money comes naturally as a result of service.Henry Ford

Mateschitz turned down repeated multi-billion-dollar offers for Red Bull because he did not want to do anything else with his life. Hetty Green, the richest woman in nineteenth-century America, wore the same black dress for forty years and lived in cheap boarding houses on either side of the Hudson River. When asked why she did not retire:

My work is my amusement.Hetty Green

Ferrari worked seven days a week into his eighties. Chung Ju-yung put studs in his old shoes to make them last longer, and was the richest man in South Korea.

Where it comes from

Most of these founders had an early experience of being underpaid for valuable work. They learned to detach the work from the immediate payment. They got good at the work itself, and the money found them. The mercenary motive is brittle. It cannot sustain twenty years of building something difficult, because the moment a comfortable offer arrives, the mercenary takes it and stops. The missionary cannot stop. They do not know what else they would do.

How to spot it

Ask why they are doing this. The mercenary will tell you about a market opportunity. The missionary will tell you about a problem they cannot stop thinking about. Andreessen and Senra agree on this distinction. Missionaries build bigger things than mercenaries, and they build them for longer.

Trait Eleven

Burn the Boats

The willingness to walk away from a local maximum in order to build something that does not yet exist.

Steve Jobs walked away from the early Apple, was forced out, came back twelve years later to a dying Apple, and rebuilt the company by killing every product line that did not fit the new vision. Henry Singleton built Teledyne into a conglomerate, and then, when the stock market peaked, bought back ninety percent of his own outstanding shares. He understood that a great founder is sometimes their own best investment.

Bill Gates killed MS-DOS for Windows. He killed Windows' priorities for the internet after writing his Internet Tidal Wave memo in 1995. He killed the internet's priorities for cloud. He has never stopped reorganising the company around what is next.

Christian Dior, at forty-one, walked away from a stable position at Lucien Lelong to launch his own house. He died ten years later, having reshaped global fashion. The most influential decade in postwar style was the one he had to burn the previous decade of his life to start.

Where it comes from

The willingness to burn the boats is usually a learned trait. The founders who have it often had an early experience of being trapped by something stable and discovering the trap was an illusion. The first burn is the hardest. Every subsequent burn is just an application of the same lesson.

How to spot it

Ask them about the most successful thing they ever quit. Have they walked away from a job, a path, a relationship that was working because they could see something better? The great founders almost always have a story like this in their twenties. The ones who do not, almost never become the great ones. People who cannot quit a comfortable local maximum cannot build the next thing, because building the next thing requires standing in the empty space between the two.

Trait Twelve

Pain Tolerance

The capacity to keep moving while suffering, for years at a time, without the suffering becoming a story about themselves.

David Senra repeats a single phrase across his entire podcast.

Excellence is the capacity to take pain.David Senra

Edison's process was suffering ten thousand failures before the light bulb worked. Knight worked his CPA day job for six years to fund Nike at night.

Beating the competition is relatively easy. Beating yourself is a never-ending commitment.Phil Knight

Walton lost his lease after five years and started over. Munger lost a child. Simons lost two children in separate accidents. They both kept working. Chung Ju-yung escaped his father five times before he made it out of the farm.

Where it comes from

Pain tolerance is built, not given. Almost every great founder had a period in childhood or adolescence where they had to endure something most people their age would not have to endure. Poverty, abuse, a sick parent, a chronic illness, an unjust school. They learned, before they could rationalise it, that suffering does not kill you. By the time the company throws its worst at them, they have been training for it for decades.

How to spot it

Look at the difficult things they have already done. Not the resume entries. The endurance entries. The athletic discipline. The long fast. The year in a hard place. The years sustaining a sick relative. The founders who have endured pain in their pre-startup life can endure it in the startup. The ones who have not, cannot, no matter how smart they are. The Senra phrase, taken from Shackleton: by endurance we conquer.

Trait Thirteen

Spikiness

Extreme strength in one or two dimensions combined with a healthy disregard for the rest. The opposite of well-roundedness.

Enzo Ferrari did not care about anything except the engines. Bugatti did not care about anything except the aesthetics. Hetty Green did not care about anything except the trade. Edison did not care about anything except invention. Buffett does not care about anything except capital allocation. Jobs did not care about anything except taste.

The well-rounded resume, the one that is competent at five things and excellent at none, is a yellow flag for founder potential. Most well-rounded candidates are optimising for institutional approval, not for greatness in any specific dimension. The great founders are the opposite. They have allowed themselves to be bad at things that do not matter to them so they can be world-class at the thing that does.

Where it comes from

Spikiness is usually a refusal. At some early point, the founder refused to round themselves out. They said no to the activities that did not interest them. They were probably punished for this socially, and they did it anyway. The refusal compounds. Twenty years of saying no to the wrong things is what produces the depth of expertise that defines the great ones.

How to spot it

Look at what they have ignored. The well-rounded candidate has neatly filled in every category. The founder candidate has gaping holes in their development next to terrifying depths of expertise. Trust the depth, not the breadth. Rounding is for diplomats. Building is for the spiky.

Trait Fourteen

Talent Magnetism

The capacity to attract people who could have worked anywhere and have chosen to work with this person.

Exceptional founders attract exceptional people. The first hire is unusually strong for the stage. The investors are unusually engaged. The advisors are unusually useful. The people around the founder will tell you, before any product metric will, whether the founder is real.

Buffett attracted Munger. Munger attracted Li Lu, the only outside investor he ever entrusted with significant capital. Jobs attracted Wozniak when both of them were teenagers, and went on to attract Jony Ive at a moment when Ive could have worked anywhere in the world. Simons attracted some of the best mathematicians and physicists alive to a strip mall on Long Island. Ferrari attracted the best racing drivers of his generation despite paying less than his rivals and being notoriously difficult to work for.

Where it comes from

Talent magnetism is a downstream effect of the other traits, not a separate trait. If a founder has the wound, the obsession, the agency, and the conviction, they pull other talented people into orbit. The best people want to work on the most important problems. If the founder is clearly working on one, they will move toward the founder. If the founder is clearly not, they will move away. The mechanism is gravitational.

How to spot it

Ask who has been around them in the last three years. Did exceptional people choose to spend time with them? Did anyone leave a stable career to work for them? Did anyone advise them for free? If yes, they have it. If no, no amount of pitch polish will fix the underlying. Talent does not gather around mediocrity. It can smell the difference from a long way off.

Trait Fifteen

Low Introspection, Bias to Action

The tendency to act, observe the result, and adjust, rather than to deliberate, plan, and refine.

A pattern that took me a long time to see because it contradicted my intuition. The great founders do not spend a lot of time examining themselves. They act, they correct, and they act again.

Andreessen has talked about this directly. One of the more surprising things, in his telling, about studying high performers is how little time they spend in self-analysis. They are not particularly self-aware. They are not unusually wise. They are running a fast loop of try, observe, adjust, try again. Most of the philosophy is done after the fact, by their biographers.

Chung Ju-yung's defining question, "Have you ever tried?", is the trait expressed in one sentence. So is Bezos's regret-minimisation framework: he imagines himself at eighty looking back, asks which option he would regret not taking, and chooses that one. The deliberation takes about ninety seconds.

Where it comes from

Low introspection is partly innate temperament and partly trained. The founders who have it have learned that action generates information faster than thought. They have also learned that introspection, past a certain point, is a form of avoidance. The people who cannot stop analysing themselves rarely build, because the analysis is comfortable in a way that building is not.

How to spot it

How quickly do they make decisions? How quickly do they recover from mistakes? Do they ruminate, or do they move? The diagnostic that has helped me most: between two meetings with a founder, did they execute on the things they said they would? The introspective candidate will have rethought the plan. The action candidate will have done it.

Trait Sixteen

The Paradox of Confidence and Doubt

The simultaneous holding of extreme self-belief and extreme self-criticism. Both, at the same time, in the same person.

The last trait is the hardest to articulate. The great founders carry an extreme confidence and an extreme self-doubt at the same time. They believe they will do something nobody has ever done, and they believe they are not enough. Both, simultaneously.

The confidence is what lets them start. The doubt is what makes them keep going.

Jobs told people their work was terrible, and he was right about most of it. He also rewrote his own keynotes thirty times the night before. McGregor predicted his fights with total conviction and trained eight hours a day because, in private, he was terrified of losing. Buffett has had several major investments fail. He still apologises in every annual letter, sometimes for things shareholders have forgotten about, because he has not. Hetty Green was the richest woman in America and lived in cheap boarding houses because she could not stop checking that the money was still there.

The Andreessen framing: a great founder is the integration of two people who should not coexist. The first thinks they are the smartest person in the room. The second is afraid they are not. The integration of these two voices is what produces the founder. One without the other is just delusion or just defeat.

Where it comes from

The wound, again. The wound produces the doubt. The work produces the confidence. Both stay. The successful founder learns to use both without being destroyed by either. This is also why so many of the great ones never seem to enjoy their success. Enjoying it would require the doubt to quiet, and the doubt is the engine.

How to spot it

Ask them what they are most proud of, and then ask them what their biggest weakness is. The honest answer to both is the founder shape. Pride without doubt is arrogance. Doubt without pride is paralysis. The integration is what you are looking for.

Conclusion

The Working Diagnostic

A summary of the sixteen traits, in order, with the diagnostic question I now use for each.

  1. Obsession. The work is involuntary. Have they put thousands of hours into their domain with no audience, no money, no encouragement?
  2. The Wound. A formative pain converted into fuel. Is there a story upstream of the work that explains the intensity?
  3. Agency. The world is wet clay. Have they already moved the world around them in small ways?
  4. First-Principles Thinking. Reasoning from base reality. When asked why something in their field is the way it is, do they build the answer from a fundamental, or do they cite a precedent?
  5. Disagreeableness. Comfort being right when the room is wrong. When pushed on a position, do they defend it or adjust?
  6. The Refusal to Quit. Endurance past the point quitting becomes rational. Have they finished anything that took longer than three years?
  7. Clock Speed and Pattern Recognition. Fast processing across a deep library. How many books have they read this year?
  8. Self-Belief Before Evidence. Precise forecast of an unearned outcome. Have they claimed something publicly that they have not yet justified?
  9. The Long Time Horizon. Playing decades-long games calmly. Can they describe a single ten-year project with patience baked in?
  10. Service as the Source of Money. Missionary, not mercenary. What is the problem they cannot stop thinking about, regardless of payment?
  11. Burn the Boats. Willingness to walk away from working things. What is the most successful thing they have ever quit?
  12. Pain Tolerance. Endurance built before the company. What hard thing have they already lived through?
  13. Spikiness. Depth in one or two dimensions, gaps elsewhere. What have they refused to round themselves out on?
  14. Talent Magnetism. Exceptional people choose to be near them. Who has chosen to spend time with them in the last three years, and why?
  15. Low Introspection, Bias to Action. Try, observe, adjust, try again. Between two meetings, did they execute on what they said they would?
  16. The Paradox of Confidence and Doubt. Pride and self-criticism, integrated. When asked what they are proud of and what their biggest weakness is, do the answers cohere into a single person?

You will not find all sixteen in any single person. The great founders have most of them, not all. The rest can be developed, found in a co-founder, or compensated for. The signal you are looking for is the combination, not any single trait in isolation. Obsession without a wound burns out. A wound without obsession becomes resentment. Self-belief without pain tolerance is delusion. Pain tolerance without self-belief is martyrdom. First-principles thinking without disagreeableness produces a person who has reached the right answer and tells no one. The combination is what builds.
